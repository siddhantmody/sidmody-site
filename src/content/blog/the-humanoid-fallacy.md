Imagine your desk after you're done working for the night. Maybe there's a notebook, a laptop, your headphones, and a calculator scattered across it. Now imagine a 7-axis, meter-long robot arm trying to clean your desk up, having never seen any of these objects before, with only a sphere to rearrange them. Rearranging just these 4 objects requires up to 19 simultaneous contact pairs being reasoned about at once, and our work did it with the simplest form of manipulation possible, a push. With 33 different objects and 98% success across 928 trials, we showed that the end effector isn't what makes manipulation special. The system has no specialized computer vision, no pretrained policy, no CAD models provided, yet it works. **If the hardest part of manipulation lived in the gripper, this shouldn't be possible.**

![A real working desk at the end of the day: notebook, laptop, headphones, calculator scattered across the surface.](/posts/manipulation/desk.jpg)

To me, robot manipulation isn't a hardware problem, it's a reasoning problem that happens to require a hardware layer to solve. The current capital flowing into robotics is betting on hardware as the path to generalist manipulation, hoping that more dexterous form factors will pay off as a robot that can complete every task thrown at it. While the bet might pay off as humanoids continue to improve and robots become more capable of unique tasks, the framing misreads where the work will truly end up. **Better hands don't solve manipulation any more than better keyboards solve writing.**

The humanoid wave is real. Figure, Apptronik, 1X, and Optimus are betting that a combination of a human-form body and a dexterous policy gets you a robot that can do anything a human can. The bet isn't wrong. The framing around it might be. Most of the discourse treats hardware as the headline - better hands, better balance, more degrees of freedom - and assumes that solving the physical layer solves manipulation. It doesn't. **Hardware doesn't create capability; it dictates scope.** A standard industrial arm constrains you to factory-floor data and structured environments. A humanoid opens up human teleop data and human-built spaces. Neither form factor, along with the countless robots that fall in between, inherently solve the problem of figuring out how to manipulate things.

Going back to the desk, let's add a layer of complexity. Your headphone wires might get tangled around your laptop, your laptop might be where the notebook usually goes, and your calculator may need to wedge between your headphone cups because it's the only space available. Even to push an object to its target, something as simple as the notebook, the arm has to figure out which surfaces are going to contact others, what the contact order looks like, the amount of force, and how to recover if one of those contact geometries behaves unexpectedly. Each pair of surfaces that could touch is a potential contact point. Each of these myriad contact points has multiple modes - separating, sticking, sliding, and more. The number of possible contact sequences balloons and quickly becomes combinatorially large. This is where the real difficulty lives. Adding fingers to the arm, especially when they have tendons and multiple joints, gives you more potential contact points, making the problem harder instead of easier. Sensors and training data help with state estimation. The underlying problem, figuring out which contacts to make in what order, is the same.

A humanoid form factor can imitate human teleoperation data, giving it the largest pool of manipulation data that we have available. It's also directly compatible with the doorways, the furniture, the infrastructure that's designed for humans. These upsides on training data and environment compatibility are real, but they don't tell you anything about capability per task. A humanoid that can imitate human teleop demos still has to generalize beyond the demos and reason about contact in novel scenes. Ultimately, the reasoning is still the bet. So what does the reasoning side of manipulation look like in practice? Here's what we built on the Push Anything project.

## what we built

![Push Anything experimental setup: a Franka Emika Panda arm with a spherical end effector pushes four objects from a cluttered configuration into target positions.](/posts/manipulation/hero.jpg)

Most manipulation algorithms work by deciding the contact sequence in advance. The engineer or a high-level planner specifies which surfaces will touch in which order, and the controller executes the plan. This works when the scene is known: assembly lines with the same parts every time, pick-and-place cells with a fixed SKU list. It breaks the moment the scene gets unfamiliar. The desk is a prime example of the failure case, with too many possible contact sequences to enumerate. Most of the objects don't have CAD models for the engineer to plan against and can change easily with something as simple as buying a new notebook or upgrading your headphones.

The alternative we worked with is to not pre-specify the contact sequence. Contact-implicit MPC (CI-MPC) adds the physics of contact directly into the optimization, with contact forces as decision variables. MPC - model predictive control - is the broader technique: solve a trajectory optimization at every timestep and replan as new sensor data comes in. Contact-implicit means contact decisions get figured out inside that optimization instead of being scripted ahead of time.

Back to the desk: in the conventional approach, you'd have to tell the arm "push the notebook here, but first move the headphones out of the way, and check if they're tangled around the calculator first." With CI-MPC, the optimizer considers all of these sequences itself, including ones the engineer would never consider enumerating. The math gets harder: you're now optimizing over decision variables that include the contact forces themselves, and each potential contact pair adds a complementarity constraint, which is the mathematical statement that contact forces only exist when surfaces are actually touching. The desk has up to 19 of those constraints active at once. The first method to make CI-MPC fast enough for real-time use was C3 (Consensus Complementarity Control, Aydinoglu et al.), which decomposed the optimization so the solver could parallelize across contact modes. It worked, but on narrow problems: single objects, known geometries, a few contacts at most. Scaling it beyond that became the focus of the Push Anything project.

We made two changes for what we called C3+. On the optimization side, we restructured the math so the solver could decouple two parts of the problem that were previously tangled together (the contact mode logic and the smooth dynamics), a small change to the formulation with big runtime consequences. This is what let the system handle scenes with up to 19 contact pairs in real time, whereas C3 was limited to a handful.

![Sampling visualization: candidate end-effector positions generated by projecting along the mesh's outward face normals, weighted by face area.](/posts/manipulation/sampling.jpg)

On the perception side, the system needed to work on objects it had never seen. We start by reconstructing a 3D mesh of any new object from a short depth-camera video. My contribution was the sampling layer that turns those reconstructed meshes into something the optimizer can use - sampling candidate end-effector positions along the mesh's face normals, weighted by face area so the MPC can focus on large stable surfaces instead of unstable corners. I also wrote the C++ extension that lets the underlying dynamics handle object-object collisions, which is what made multi-object scenes tractable.

The result: 33 unfamiliar objects, 98% success rate across 928 trials, multi-object decluttering scenes with up to 4 objects. The generalization isn't coming from a hand or from more degrees of freedom. It's coming from the contact reasoning. Same arm, same controller, same sphere - given any new object, the system reconstructs its geometry, samples candidate contact points, and solves for the contact sequence that pushes it to the goal. **The hardware is incidental to whether the system works on a new object.**

## the broader pattern

Sphere-and-contact-reasoning isn't a special case. It's the same pattern that shows up across recent work on manipulation generalization. Whether the system is a model-based controller like C3+, a learned policy like Diffusion Policy or π0, or something hybrid, the part doing the generalization work is the contact reasoning. The companies and labs making real progress on manipulation right now have this in common: they've found different ways to encode contact understanding, and that's what their systems generalize on.

The same logic applies on the specialized side. The robots I'm headed to build this summer at Cosmic Robotics are designed for solar panel manipulation in field conditions. The form factor is specific because the domain is: solar fields don't need humanoid bodies, so the robots don't have them. But the manipulation reasoning still has to be solved. Solar panels come in many shapes and weights, field conditions are noisy, contact failures happen at scale. Picking a specialized form factor lets the team skip humanoid balance problems and teleop rigs, but it doesn't skip the physics. The reasoning problem is the actual research problem, just inside a tighter scope.

![Cosmic Robotics solar panel manipulation robots deployed in field conditions. A specialized form factor matched to a specific domain.](/posts/manipulation/cosmic.jpg)

One common argument is that a lot of the promising current work in manipulation is data-driven, not model-based. Diffusion Policy, π0, RT-2 are learning contact behavior from demonstration data rather than encoding it as physics. I think this is a genuinely promising direction and possibly the dominant one over the next decade as data acquisition scales and sim results improve. The data, however, has to capture contact correctly. The simulators that generate training data have to model contact correctly. The teleoperation rigs that collect demonstrations have to record forces and torques accurately enough to be useful. Physical Intelligence isn't spending hundreds of millions on data collection infrastructure by accident, they're spending it because the contact physics has to live somewhere in the pipeline, and for them it lives in the data. C3+ is one bet on where the physics lives. Foundation model approaches are a different bet. Both rest on the same recognition: respecting the physics is the actual work.

**Form factor decides scope. Algorithm decides where contact understanding lives.** Neither alone gets you a generalist robot. The companies that solve manipulation in the next decade are going to be the ones that get the reasoning right within their chosen scope, whatever hardware they choose. That's the actual race, and the form factor wars between humanoid and specialized robots or even individual humanoid robots are a distraction from it.

I'm spending the next few years inside this question. The research in the DAIR lab is one piece of it, utilizing physics-grounded models and explicit contact dynamics, the C3+ work. Cosmic Robotics this summer is another, building specialized robots in real field conditions. I'll also track learned manipulation across the industry from the outside and remain deeply invested, both through people I know in the field and the papers coming out of it.

The next decade will probably tell us which is closest to right. It'll also tell us whether specialized robots end up eating more of the long tail than the humanoid wave is currently planning for. The reasoning is the bet, but whoever wins out will dictate where it lives.
